Sic.

This is latin for thus it is or so it is. It is usually employed in records to display dialogue is written verbatim. I am employing this term in a more … progressive way.

See, it is clear if you're reading this that this is the first post of this blog that I assume you're interested in. And you may find yourself kind of annoyed that it is the way it is: about nothing in particular, or about the particular in nothing in this instance. It is that way and usually will be because for any other written work in history, the beginning was quite important. To find the written word treated so casually, to describe all denominations of things from pure information to the mundanity of civil life, and without the privacy of it being your own or of someone long gone, it is inherently in conflict with the nature of written work.

Better stated: this form of writing is, unlike any other, very material; It is inseparable from the author, and thus very revealing. In all of history, the greatest texts clearly wanted to be ethereal — devoid of human taint — because the greatest texts(scripture) were, and still are, devoid of human taint.

However, this form of writing is the antithesis of esotericism, informal and personal. It is at odds with our natural method of analysing texts. This is simultaneously the worst and the best part of it. Thus, the best word to describe any blog is sic; written thus, written with all its mistakes, written undeniably by a human. The best meaning of that word, sic, is so it is, as in: so it is, and let it be. Why so? Because it is not beholden to anything, so it is. Let it be.

1. Modern and ancient writing

In all of art and culture, there has been a movement of trying to embrace a modern perspective: throwing away baroque traditions in order to explore the possibilities of the future. I think in our time of the internet, even outside of blogs, things are very informal. The newspaper was already the first entry into modern texts. It has all the right strangeness about it.

The blog is the final continuation upon this line. It is crossbreeded from the diary, the textbook, the newspaper, the commentary, and most importantly: the spoken word.

1.1. A new medium

As the printing press came about, a greater variety of written works came about as we afforded more kinds of writing that were not worthy of being written and proofed by scribes. Now, with the evolution of the internet, we are similarly finding not only more minimal writing, but also finding a greater range of expression than the written word itself, and the name for the medium of the elusive blog is hypertext.

This is not to say that works that aren't worthy of being bound into a paperback are irrelevant. Just as the written works of the printing press, even though less in denomination than the theses of ancient times — which were less in denomination than scripture — wasn't irrelevant, so too is hypertext relevant. Our news is presented to us via hypertext, our letters, our books — we even trade in hypertext! Clearly, the more modern medium is more relevant because it is more fit for its time than the ancient medium.

I also believe that the medium of hypertext — that is, all that is encountered in the hypertext transfer protocol — is so malleable that it will be the final medium of writing for humanity. There will probably be no successor to it, because hypertext will adapt to become whatever wants to usurp it. This is significant because the medium that we use will govern what we think. Thus, when we have a universal medium for expression, the communication of thoughts will be best presented, and the speed at which humanity develops will accelerate into infinity. Hypertext is as close as I can think now, because it unites old media and new media(of what it can) together into one.

Some might argue a medium that is universal simply because it contains other media within it is cheating universality. I challenge the reader to present a medium that is universal and homoiconic.

1.2. The modern text

The introduction of modern writing will be characterised by the unity between the spoken word and the written word, for better or for worse. In approaching a universal medium, inevitably, every form of expression will be appraised to the same level by the medium.

This has the strength in that the modern text is dynamic. It is beholden to nothing at all, and so has all the wit of the spoken word, whilst being ever-challenging as a written work. It can switch between the tact of slang, the eloquence of verse, and the clarity of the written word almost instantly, and can become all 3 simultaneously.

The modern text, thanks to the means of the internet, is constantly evolving. It is not so one monolith, but a simultaneous cavalcade of interpretations of the medium. It is all it could have ever been all at once. It is unlike any other previous media because it has both the vectors of expression of the text content and the text itself.

In theory this is what it is, but I hope its development will be fruitful. It's upon bloggers, as the authors of extended texts of this modern text, the modern medium of hypertext, to develop upon what kind of self expression is possible. How close can we bring the symbol to the reality, the painting to its subject, and the human language to the human thought?

The modern text's nature means that we will have to evaluate every article's importance on our own. Nothing will be sacred, but then what shall happen to the sacred text?

Thanks to the internet's plasticity, nothing is ever lost. Every text is preserved and it hasn't been easier to preserve it in its state. Sacred text will never see death, nor will it see vandalism. There will always be a clean copy.

I'd like to believe that we will naturally choose to appraise sacred texts individually, even with our texts being appraised systematically.

…

This is by far, the most amazing part of blogs. It is such a disappointment that we've only been willing to scratch the surface of self expression in this medium. This is just part of what makes it so interesting. It's weakness is its strength.

2. Outdated tradition

Personal websites, in the year of our lord 2026, are not a common thing. The dot-com bubble was a couple decades ago, and we still feel the effects: nobody really bothers consolidating their presence on the internet. So it becomes necessary for me to explain why personal websites still matter, as I represent this slowly closing group of users.

Do they matter? For the majority, absolutely not. I could pretend all day that anybody and everybody must create a personal website, and share their opinions, and must be worthwhile, and must have something useful to share, but these are fantasies one could lull themselves to sleep with and certainly not the reality. Not everybody needs to do it, thus all who do shall honour it.

Then in this position, I should justify why I find myself worthy of such an endeavour. I'd say firstly because I have enough worthwhile things to share, and because I am confident that they are worth sharing, and because my opinions are worth sharing. If in no way else, then in this.

How can I explain if it should matter to you? The only way to reach the end of a road is to walk through it.

2.1. Social networking sites replaced personal websites

I don't like saying ugly things, and it isn't polite to anyway. We could always use the opportunity to honour beautiful things instead of insulting ugly things. We become uglier than what we insult in that case.

With that said, maintaining a personal website isn't popular for good reason. It requires thought, patience, perserverance, dedication. It also grew irrelevant over time. I can outline that personal websites fell out of fashion because (1) it was inconvenient for anybody who wasn't a hacker, and always will be, (2) its purposes didn't overlap with the requirements and expectations of people, exemplified by that (3) nobody had things worthwhile to share, and as such weren't willing to waste their time for things even they consider insignificant.

Social networking sites grew in that condition to provide instant messaging, discussions, entertainment, and sharing, all in ways that have no relation to personal websites whatsoever, besides of course, the 'sharing' part.

Eventually, the time came that these hubs of global interaction became such a great monolith that they began replacing the features of the regular distributed uses of the internet — among them, the personal website — with convenient, simplified alternatives. Social networking sites took all the benefits of the personal website and aptly put them into the paradigm of a global spitoon. Hence, when all the needs of the layman from the internet are met in one place, why ever so pay attention to anything else?

This presents the point I wanted to show: nobody ever cared about personal websites. At least, no layman cared about personal websites. A brief stint in time, people were experimenting with them through blogs, and when that gained interest, it was eventually swallowed into the social networking paradigm, and they returned to being quite irrelevant and outdated for all they served.

So why, really, why should anybody care, layman or not?

2.2. Things personal websites are good for, and things to which they aren't

In the modern day.

Here are some points I have for this:

They're good for professionals.
Anybody who is exemplar in their field usually has a personal website, mainly to display their achievements and possibly share knowledge with others. This is a justified use, both for them and for the community.
They're not good for hobbyists.
If you are doing it 'for fun' and with no particular goal in mind, nobody will really care, neither you. This is at best a secondary reason. Websites aren't toys, they're more like notebooks.
They are good for self expression
In their traditional sense, they are definitely for self expression. Sharing your opinions, your interests without worry for how it may integrate into anything else. Some kinds of things simply don't fit into social media.
They are your own
In legal sense, they are your property. You copyright all your writings under your name, you own the space that you put the website on, and you may do whatsoever you like with it. That's what is nice about it. It explains why it isn't popular among people, because not much people know what to do with a website. But for those that have an idea, it is invaluable.

2.3. Very simple

In the end, a website is simply a technology, a tool. It is only useful insofar as one makes use of it. It is 'out of fashion' because many have learned they don't like using tools of this kind, and want to use others. Just because a hammer looks nice, doesn't mean that we should use it for everything; Just because a personal website is appealing, doesn't mean that we should replace everything with it.

And in context of the previous paragraph, all that we said seems useless, but it wasn't, because it allowed us to reach this conclusion. This is why it matters, because it is useful. Nothing more, nothing less. We make of this technology what we will.

3. Conclusion

And so I close this essay starting my blog with a commentary on the very medium that I am using, as a fitting start. It isn't good. We have presented this text to you verbatim, with all its flaws. Make of it what you will.

I think the most fitting name for this blog is sic. It deserves that name. We shall call this amazing contemporary commentary serial essay, guide, everything-text sic, for we have presented it thus.
